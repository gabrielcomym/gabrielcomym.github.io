Kedro — Modular Pipelines

Overview

Kedro Modular Pipelines was a product design initiative focused on making a technically complex concept easier to understand, navigate and communicate inside Kedro.

Kedro is an open-source Python framework for creating reproducible, maintainable and modular data science code. It borrows concepts from software engineering and applies them to machine-learning workflows, including modularity, separation of concerns and versioning. Kedro is hosted by the Linux Foundation AI & Data.

The project centered on visualizing modular pipelines, a concept already embedded in the codebase, in a way that felt intuitive for technical users across different seniority levels. The goal was not only to represent the structure accurately, but to simplify complexity and improve storytelling for teams and clients.

The Problem

Modular pipelines were already part of the Kedro codebase, but the concept was hard to visualize clearly.

This created a product design challenge:

  • the underlying structure was technically complex
  • users across different seniority levels needed to understand and use it
  • the visualization had to support clarity without oversimplifying the system
  • the product needed to help teams tell better stories about pipelines and their structure

The core challenge was how to make a dense technical concept legible, intuitive and useful inside the product experience.

Why It Mattered

The work was strategically important because Kedro aimed to simplify complexity and improve storytelling around technical workflows.

If modular pipelines could be visualized well, the product would become more useful in several ways:

  • users could understand pipeline structures faster
  • teams could navigate complexity with less friction
  • clients and collaborators could follow the logic of the system more easily
  • Kedro could turn a complex technical capability into a more accessible product feature

This made the project both a usability problem and a product communication problem.

It also mattered in the context of Kedro’s wider growth as an open-source platform. As the product expanded its user base and enterprise relevance, making complex capabilities easier to understand had value not only for day-to-day use, but also for adoption, collaboration and confidence in the framework.

Discovery and Research

The project started with a foundation-building phase focused on understanding the space before moving into solution design.

This stage included:

  • desk research
  • assumption validation
  • subject familiarization
  • engagement with domain specialists
  • engagement with hero users
  • data review
  • participation in technical discussions

The goal was to build a deeper understanding of the problem space and avoid designing a visualization based only on surface-level assumptions.

Planning and Alignment

The project relied heavily on collaborative work across product, design and technical stakeholders.

An important part of the process was creating alignment around:

  • what modular pipelines meant in practice
  • which user needs mattered most
  • where technical constraints would shape the solution
  • how the team could explore broadly before narrowing to viable directions

This alignment work created the conditions for faster decision-making during ideation and prototyping.

Key Insights

Several product insights emerged from the process:

  • the difficulty was not only visual complexity, but conceptual complexity
  • users needed structure and familiarity in the way information was grouped and navigated
  • collaboration between design and engineering was essential because the concept was tightly tied to technical implementation
  • the product needed to support both understanding and communication, not just raw exploration

These insights pushed the work beyond flowchart styling and toward a more intentional navigation and grouping system.

Product and Design Decisions

The project translated discovery and collaboration into a clear set of product and design decisions:

  • focus on an intuitive visualization of modular pipelines rather than a purely literal representation
  • optimize node visualization and grouping to make flowcharts easier to interpret
  • introduce features such as focus mode to reduce complexity during exploration
  • use tree-style navigation to create a more familiar and folder-like mental model
  • refine concepts with engineering to balance future-facing ideas with what was viable in the timeframe

These decisions made the product more understandable without disconnecting it from the underlying system.

Ideation and Validation

Collaborative ideation was a central part of the project.

Workshops brought together product teams, data engineers and scientists to support co-creation in a process designed to be:

  • inclusive
  • engaging
  • collaborative
  • safe for creative exploration

This phase included:

  • ideation workshops
  • initial concepts
  • prioritisation
  • user stories

During design exploration, the work intentionally pushed current technical limits to open conversations with the development team about future possibilities. From there, ideas were refined to focus on what was realistic within the available timeframe.

After exploration, clickable prototypes were brought to users and tested through quick usability sessions. This helped validate concepts and refine the direction before final delivery.

Validation methods included:

  • user interviews
  • usability testing

Delivery

After extensive testing and validation, final polished assets were delivered in Figma ready for development.

The delivery phase focused on turning the validated concept into a build-ready solution with:

  • optimized node visualization
  • grouping systems for flowcharts
  • focus mode
  • tree-style navigation
  • polished assets ready for engineering handoff

The key delivery challenge was overcoming technical constraints while still simplifying complex data structures in a way that improved usability and collaboration.

End-to-End Process Summary

The full process can be summarized as:

  1. Build understanding through desk research, assumption validation and subject familiarization.
  2. Engage domain specialists and hero users.
  3. Review data and join technical discussions to deepen problem understanding.
  4. Align the team around the problem space and technical reality.
  5. Run collaborative ideation workshops with product, data and engineering stakeholders.
  6. Generate concepts, frame opportunities and prioritize directions.
  7. Explore ideas through sketching, conceptual design and prototyping.
  8. Push technical boundaries during exploration, then refine toward feasible solutions.
  9. Validate concepts with users through interviews and usability testing.
  10. Deliver polished assets in Figma ready for development.

Lessons from the Project

This case reinforces a few product design lessons:

  • visualizing technical systems requires solving for conceptual clarity, not only visual clarity
  • collaborative ideation is especially valuable when the problem space sits between product, engineering and specialist users
  • familiarity in interaction patterns can help users understand technically dense structures faster
  • strong product design in technical tools often comes from balancing ambition with implementation reality
