WovenLight — Rapid Diagnostic Reports

Overview

Rapid Diagnostic Reports was a product design initiative focused on transforming an early internal tool into a scalable and user-centered product for Private Equity workflows.

WovenLight is a hybrid investment and technology firm that uses data analytics and AI to maximize value creation across portfolio companies throughout the investment lifecycle. I joined in early 2024 as the company’s first Principal Designer, working with a team of engineers and finance specialists to shape the product direction, interaction model and overall user experience of its internal tools.

The Rapid Diagnostic Reports product sat at the center of that effort. The goal was to use multi-agent LLM interactions to streamline workflows for deployment managers and analysts, support faster value creation and turn the product into both a practical internal tool and a strong commercial asset.

Role and Scope

As Principal Designer at WovenLight, my broader responsibility included product leadership and vision, end-to-end product design, design direction and branding.

For Rapid Diagnostic Reports, the scope included:

  • workshop facilitation
  • creative ideation
  • team dynamics and cross-functional collaboration
  • Crazy 8's
  • prioritisation
  • design system creation and application
  • design QA
  • product vision
  • user interviews
  • requirements gathering
  • concept validation
  • prototyping
  • user testing
  • product architecture

The work also sat inside a wider design leadership scope that included:

  • design leadership and vision in a 20-person startup
  • strategy and prioritisation with product and technical directors
  • OKRs setting and tracking metrics
  • close collaboration with ML engineers, finance specialists and technical users across different seniority levels
  • establishing feedback loops and ways of working centered on collaboration, creativity and momentum

The Problem

When I joined the company in early 2024, I inherited an internal tool that was hard-coded, lacked scalability and overlooked fundamental user experience principles.

The product had several structural issues:

  • difficult navigation
  • branding inconsistencies
  • weak information architecture
  • limited scalability
  • limited flexibility for future features
  • lack of clear product vision
  • poor alignment with software engineering standards

This undermined both efficiency and user satisfaction across the organization.

Why It Mattered

The project mattered because the tool was directly connected to how WovenLight created and communicated value.

It was not only an internal workflow product. It also had strategic importance across:

  • internal appraisal and deployment workflows
  • speed and efficiency in value creation
  • commercial storytelling with clients
  • WovenLight’s perceived technical capability
  • IP and product value creation

If the product remained brittle and hard to use, it would limit both internal effectiveness and external commercial impact. If redesigned well, it could become a flagship product and a storefront for the company’s AI capabilities.

Discovery and Research

The first phase focused on refining the problem and understanding where AI-driven automation could create meaningful value in Private Equity.

To build that foundation, I:

  • collaborated with industry experts and product managers
  • gathered assumptions
  • defined key questions to be answered
  • introduced stakeholders to design thinking
  • created discussion guides for user interviews

This established a user-centered research approach and aligned the team around the right discovery questions before solution work began.

In the broader discovery phase, I interviewed Private Equity professionals across levels of seniority to understand:

  • workflows
  • tools
  • pain points
  • expectations
  • opportunities for automation

The output of this phase included:

  • workflow blueprints
  • personas
  • opportunity mapping
  • a clearer problem statement
  • an internal audit of product needs and technical challenges

This helped align user needs and business goals before feature design and architecture decisions were made.

Planning and Alignment

An important part of the project was aligning product, design and technical stakeholders around a shared direction.

This included:

  • assumptions gathering
  • question framing
  • planning sessions
  • alignment on user-centered methods
  • collaboration with the product manager, CTO and team leads

This phase was critical because the product needed both a new design vision and a stronger architectural direction. Without alignment, the team would have risked shipping isolated UI improvements instead of a scalable product foundation.

Key Insights

The discovery work surfaced several important product signals:

  • the existing tool had product value, but its current implementation was too brittle to scale
  • the real opportunity was not just automation, but workflow transformation
  • users needed clearer information architecture and more reliable interaction patterns
  • agentic experiences required strong UX guidance to feel trustworthy and usable
  • commercial value and internal workflow value were tightly connected in the same product

These insights reframed the product from a rough internal tool into a strategic product platform.

Product and Design Decisions

The project translated discovery into a series of product and design decisions:

  • redesign the tool as a scalable and modular product instead of extending the original hard-coded implementation
  • integrate software engineering principles into the product direction to support future-proofing
  • define a clearer product vision and architecture before expanding features
  • create agentic interaction patterns that were usable, understandable and operationally valuable
  • improve information architecture and data organization to support better workflows
  • design prompt and query experiences that helped users ask better questions
  • create meaningful feedback mechanisms to increase transparency into agent reasoning
  • build custom components and UX patterns for agent-based interactions instead of relying only on generic UI conventions

These decisions ensured the product evolved as both a better workflow tool and a stronger technical asset.

Ideation and Validation

During ideation, I synthesized interview findings into a clear problem statement and worked with the team to define solution directions.

This phase included:

  • creative ideation
  • design thinking workshops
  • Crazy 8's
  • sketching
  • personas
  • user journeys
  • workflow mapping
  • prioritisation

Working closely with the product manager, CTO and team leads, we prioritized features by impact and feasibility to define a focused roadmap.

During exploration, I pushed the product vision toward a scalable and future-proof system while staying grounded in real technical constraints. The work emphasized:

  • user-agent dynamics
  • cognitive frameworks for agent interaction
  • prompt engineering support for users
  • transparency and feedback patterns for agent behavior
  • iterative prototyping informed by user feedback

Clickable prototypes were then used in user feedback sessions and client demos to validate the direction and strengthen commercial confidence in the product.

Delivery

The delivery phase focused on shipping quickly without losing structural quality.

To support MVP timelines and upcoming investment opportunities, I worked closely with engineering to align the design system and product delivery approach. The team adopted MUI as a practical code base to accelerate development while custom components were designed for agentic workflows and unique interaction patterns.

The delivery work included:

  • design system work
  • technical delivery collaboration
  • weekly design reviews
  • technical sessions
  • custom component design
  • design QA
  • design debt backlog management in Jira

I also provided active dev support by reviewing the product in staging and development environments, identifying quick wins, logging design debt and helping prioritize improvements under time and technical constraints.

For data and usage tracking, the team used PostHog.

End-to-End Process Summary

The full process can be summarized as:

  1. Assess the inherited tool and identify structural product issues.
  2. Gather assumptions and define key research questions.
  3. Align stakeholders around a user-centered design approach.
  4. Interview Private Equity users across different roles and seniority levels.
  5. Create workflow blueprints, personas and opportunity maps.
  6. Define a clear problem statement and synthesize discovery findings.
  7. Run ideation workshops and prioritize opportunities by impact and feasibility.
  8. Define a scalable product vision, architecture and UX direction.
  9. Explore agentic interaction patterns through sketches and prototypes.
  10. Validate concepts through user sessions and client demos.
  11. Deliver the MVP through close engineering collaboration, custom components, QA and design debt management.

Lessons from the Project

This case reinforces a few product design lessons:

  • inheriting a weak internal tool can be a product opportunity if the underlying workflow has strategic value
  • agentic systems need strong UX patterns to be understandable, trustworthy and useful
  • product architecture and UX architecture need to evolve together in AI products
  • internal tools can create outsized business impact when they also support commercial storytelling and client confidence
